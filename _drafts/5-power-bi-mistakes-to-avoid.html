5 Power BI Mistakes to avoid

Introduction

[dropcap]O[/dropcap]ne day, a Power BI User asked me: “Which are the mistakes I should avoid.” Although the tool is permissive and allows a lot of madness, there are indeed a few points that seem to be important to mention.

While writing, many ideas came to me. So I limited the number to 5 to keep only those who seem to be the most relevant.

1. Do not structure your data with the Query Builder

Power BI offers users to load the data quickly and does not require them to use the Query Builder to perform transformations.

Unless you already have a data warehouse, this step is crucial.

 

Why do we need to transform our data:

  • Filter out unnecessary information
    • Limit the information that would not make sense in your model and, therefore, in your reports.
    • Limit the number of lines to optimize the display time of your visuals
  • Remove unnecessary attributes
    • Less information, more your model will be understandable for your users.
  • Remove duplicated data. (Use the GROUP BY function)
  • Rename attributes
    • Give an understandable name for your users.
  • Datatyping your attributes
  • Use a database as a source
    • With larger volumes, and when you have a database, feel free to use SQL to perform the most demanding tasks.
    • I am thinking of filters, GROUP BY, joins, and limiting the necessary attributes.

 

2. Don’t take care of your data model

I covered in my last post some tips for creating a more suitable data model.

If your data model is simple, structured, ordered, your users will be able to use it! At the same time, when you will develop your DAX measures or design your reports, you'll be the first satisfied with the work done upstream.

 

  • Give a logical and understandable meaning to users
  • Denormalize your model
  • Make tables reusable

 

I invite you to read my article: https://lazysnail.net/en/power-bi-your-model-matters/

 

3. Do not dwell on understanding the DAX language

Programming in DAX is becoming Harry Potter !! On the Internet, all posts will sell you dream! The true Magic with Power BI operates only with beautiful DAX measures.

Yes, it's true!

But in the beginning, apart from the scar and Harry's wand (to scrape your back), the magic does not operate.

There are some concepts to understand, and I find that it is much less intuitive than learning C #, PHP, JavaScript … (Some people will quote me the MDX, I will be happy to ignore them !! :) )

Do not be discouraged; in the end, it’s very simple!

Here are some tips which helped me to understand and learned the language.

 

  • Capitalize on what you have just done
    • Maintain a Power BI with your documented examples. I carried out this process, and I use this file daily for my new requests. [You will find on this page the file I made: I named it LazyDAX]
  • Use variables in your measures (Much more clear, and easy. It was a game-changer for me)
  • Understand the concept of CONTEXT TRANSITION
  • Forget your SQL knowledge
    • Are you a Hard SQL Coder? Then you need to think “out of the box”. This is time to think less and keep the focus on your results.
    • Excel users without programming experience seem to be more confident.
  • Measures only.
    • Do not create calculated columns or tables in DAX. (Prefer the query builder or SQL to create them). It’s more performant.
    • Measures and columns don’t share the same logic (At least for me)

 

4. Duplicate reports

For IT services, " self-service BI" means:

  • Data duplication
  • Multiplication of files
  • Maintainability not under the control

 

Feel free to apply some rigor from the beginning.

Make sure you will be able to understand what you have achieved in the coming months.

Reuse your datasets in several reports via the option Get Data> Power BI datasets.

 

5. Put a lot of information on a page

 

Less is More!

 

When we spent time on our model, our measures, and data exploration, we tend to serve all the information to the users.

That may be interesting, but unless your goal is to reproduce the dashboard of an Airbus (or Boeing), too much information hurts the understanding of your reports.

 

Feel the user experience and offer them the satisfaction to understand the information quickly.

For more dynamism, you can add your other information via a drill through or a tooltip.
